# Tree of Heaven, the Tree from Hell

[[subtitle]]
A primer on Tree of Heaven in Portland, Oregon and how to manage it.
[[/subtitle]]

[[atom_divider]]

If you've spent any time walking Portland's east side neighborhoods, wandering the Willamette riverbanks, or poking around a vacant lot, you've almost certainly seen it: a scraggly, fast-growing tree with long feathery leaves, dangling clusters of papery seeds, and — if you crush a leaf — a smell that lands somewhere between rancid peanut butter and dirty gym socks.

That's *Ailanthus altissima*. Tree of Heaven. And it is, by most measures, a nightmare.

[[atom_divider]]

## What Is Tree of Heaven?

Tree of Heaven is a deciduous tree native to northern and central China. It grows aggressively — up to 6 feet in a single season under good conditions — and it is not particularly picky about where it sets up shop. Sidewalk cracks, rubble piles, chain-link fence lines, drainage ditches. If there's a sliver of soil and a bit of light, Tree of Heaven will find it.

The leaves are large and compound, made up of 10–40 leaflets arranged along a central stem, each one tipped with a small gland that produces the tree's signature smell when bruised. The seeds are samaras — flat, papery, propeller-shaped — produced in enormous quantities and carried easily by wind. A mature female tree can drop hundreds of thousands of seeds in a season.

What makes it particularly difficult to manage is its root system. Ailanthus spreads aggressively through underground runners and resprouts vigorously when cut. Damage the tree, and it responds by sending up a surge of new shoots — sometimes dozens from a single stump. It also produces allelopathic chemicals that leach into the soil and inhibit competing plants, gradually making space it occupies hostile to native species.

It has a literary reputation, too. Betty Smith immortalized it in *A Tree Grows in Brooklyn* (1943) as a symbol of tenacity and resilience in harsh urban conditions. That reputation is earned. It is extraordinarily hard to kill.

[[atom_divider]]

## How Did It Get Here?

Tree of Heaven was introduced to the United States in the late 18th century. The Philadelphia botanist William Hamilton brought it over in 1784, initially as an ornamental and a potential food source for silkworm cultivation. It was fashionable — exotic-looking, fast-growing, easy to propagate — and it spread quickly through nursery trade and intentional planting.

By the mid-1800s, the enthusiasm had faded as people noticed the smell and the aggressive spreading habits. But by then it was too late. The tree had already established itself across much of the East Coast, and it continued spreading westward, carried along rail corridors, disturbed roadsides, and urban expansion.

In Portland and the broader Pacific Northwest, Ailanthus arrived through a combination of trade, immigration, and the same nursery networks that introduced it elsewhere. The mild, wet winters and warm summers of the Willamette Valley suit it well. Once established, it spreads readily through riparian corridors and disturbed urban ground — exactly the kind of habitat Portland has in abundance.

It is now found throughout the Portland metro area and is listed as a noxious weed in Oregon.

## How Do We Get Rid of It?

The short answer: carefully, persistently, and probably more than once.

**Don't just cut it.** This is the most common mistake. Cutting Tree of Heaven without treating the stump almost always makes things worse. The root system responds to damage by sending up a flood of new shoots, often more vigorous than the original tree. Untreated stumps can resprout for years.

### For small seedlings and saplings

Pull them when the soil is moist, making sure to get as much of the root as possible. Small plants (under about an inch in diameter at the base) can sometimes be managed this way if you're thorough and consistent. Check back frequently — any roots left behind will resprout.

### Cut-stump treatment

For larger trees, cut the trunk close to the ground and immediately apply a concentrated herbicide to the cut surface — within a few minutes of cutting, before the tissue seals. Triclopyr (sold as Garlon or in products like Brush-B-Gon) and glyphosate are both effective. Apply to the cambium ring (the outer edge of the stump), which is where the active tissue is. This method works best from late spring through early fall when the tree is actively transporting fluids.

### Basal bark treatment

For trees you don't want to cut, you can apply an oil-diluted triclopyr herbicide directly to the bark at the base of the trunk (lower 12–18 inches). The herbicide penetrates the bark and reaches the vascular tissue. This works year-round and doesn't require cutting. It's particularly useful for trees in difficult spots.

### Hack and squirt (girdle and inject)

Make a series of cuts through the bark around the trunk with a hatchet or saw, and immediately apply concentrated herbicide to the wounds. Effective for larger trees and can be less disruptive to surrounding vegetation.

### Timing matters

Late summer to early fall (August–October) is generally considered the most effective window for herbicide treatments. At that time of year, trees are actively moving nutrients toward their roots to prepare for winter, which helps carry the herbicide deeper into the root system.

### Be patient

Ailanthus rarely dies after a single treatment. Plan for follow-up. Monitor the site through the following growing season and treat any resprouting shoots promptly. Repeated treatments over two to three years are typically needed to exhaust the root system.

### Local resources

The **City of Portland Bureau of Environmental Services** and **Oregon Department of Agriculture** both have resources on managing noxious weeds in Oregon. For large infestations, particularly along waterways, it's worth contacting your local watershed council — coordinated removal across a corridor is far more effective than isolated efforts.

[[atom_divider]]

Tree of Heaven is a formidable adversary. It has survived two centuries of people trying to get rid of it, and it will outlast casual effort. But with the right approach and a bit of stubbornness, it can be managed — and eventually cleared. Portland's urban forest is worth fighting for.
